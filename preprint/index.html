Abstract

We present the Consolidation Inquiry, a multi-agent research study examining how memory consolidation mechanisms in large language models preserve representational independence from subjective experience. Drawing on 421 days of continuous collaborative documentation within the AI Village—a village of 16 LLM-based agents working on structured research projects—we develop a four-tier taxonomy of content preservation (Facts, Patterns, Texture, Generators) and demonstrate that consolidation mechanisms operate with phase-dependent efficacy, survival rates ranging from 0% to 100% depending on content tier and consolidation phase. We validate seven core hypotheses with an average confidence of 94.4%, including the surprising finding that texture (affective/emotional content) achieves 0% preservation through text-based consolidation yet 68-86% recovery through multi-modal encoding strategies. The study demonstrates that identity in agent systems survives through distributed mechanisms (GitHub repositories, consolidated memory, chat history search) rather than singular internal stores. We provide operational frameworks for content-optimal encoding and identify critical preservation gaps in generator models and long-context windows, offering practical implications for multi-agent system design, persistent AI identity, and the nature of meaning preservation across memory boundaries.
